What is the opposition to this?

Considering the fact that our world is adopting new technologies at an ever-rapid rate, it would seem that our education system would be open to their use in the classroom. Unfortunately this is not always the case. Presenting at an education forum at Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 2013, educational innovator Hayal Koksal, PhD stated, "Without doubt, instructional technology and its potential to improve instruction have progressed more rapidly than acceptance and utilization in the classroom" (Koksal, 2). Dr. Koksal spoke of a long history of teacher opposition to new technologies when he quoted another educational innovator W.F. Van Wyck who, speaking at a similar forum in 1976, stated, "The largest single factor affecting adoption is teacher resistance" (Koksal, 2). Some of the resistance sounds noble; teachers do not want to lose the human touch and give their students over to computer screens and video lessons. But much of the resistance is more systemic, involving cultural issues within individual schools and administrations. In a study done at two universities and reported in the journal Issues in Education, the author summarized resistance discovered when new technology was introduced to faculty. The researchers identified four barriers to teacher's adoption of the new technology. The first two are roadblocks educators themselves should be familiar with: lack of interest and lack of motivation on the part of the teachers after the new technology was presented. The final two are more deeply challenging; the author reports, "Third, and something familiar to all organizations, implementing change is a common psychoanalytic conundrum, which represents a mix of personalities and quirky behaviors. Finally, recognizing the existence of a condition that is far from equilibrium, which state is inherent in a dynamic environment like academia" (Moerschell). Educational institutions of all sizes become mini-communities with cultures all their own, where the individuals seem to cling to the status quo. Convincing teachers to adopt a new technology presents several challenges, which include, " infrastructure, expert knowledge, training, long-term vision, cost-benefit equations, and sufficient utilization to sustain the change" (Moerschell). At Clintondale, not all the teachers were enthusiastic about the new system that required them to put their lessons in video form. As one teacher described his reticence, "I was intimidated by another math instructor's video being seen by my students, but then I found that material presented from a different perspective is quite beneficial to them" (Frenkel). Overcoming both administrative and teacher resistance to embracing new technology is clearly a big challenge to educational reform.

Adding to the complexity is the fact that the expectations for American teachers are at the mercy of ever-changing political whims. In recent history, the No Child Left Behind Act mandated standardized testing for students with potentially dire consequences for teachers who failed to achieve standards; but there were also reforms in response to international events such as World Wars and the Russian launch of Sputnik (Horn). The constantly changing pressures on educators have left some weary of change itself, and "they view educational reform as a phenomenon characterized by one-shot, quick-fix, and faddish solutions that are here today and gone tomorrow" (Horn, 259). These frustrations have led technology-adopting education reformers to come up with strategies for introducing change. Tom Haymes, a Director of Technology and Instructional Computing at small college proposes that new technologies must fulfill the "three E's" to be adopted; they must be evident, easy to use and essential. (Haymes, 67-68) Teachers, he argues, must embrace technology for the same reasons the rest of the public does, because technology eases burdens and enriches life experience in user-friendly way.
