What are the approches to online learning

Considering the challenging socioeconomic factors, personal barriers of individual teachers, political climate changes and cultural forces that affect traditional classroom-based education, the simplicity and ease of access to online learning outside of the traditional model comes across as profound. Just as I discovered the online options to enhance my understanding of computer science junior year, students like Ryan Orbuch and Michael Hansen, recently featured in a NY Times article, are finding ways to develop usable skills online. Orbuch and Hansen, high school seniors, created an app using skills learned online, then marketed the product and it became a bestseller. Sometimes working on the app instead of schoolwork, "they were at it hours every day refining the design, with Michael writing thousands of lines of code using Objective-C, a computer language that he learned from online tutorials" (Richtell, 3). Their app earned them thirty thousand dollars in the first year since its launch (Richtell, 4). These teen's success demonstrates the ability of online learning to enhance and enrich education beyond what a traditional classroom could and would have taught.

Salman Khan discovered the possibility for technology enhancing education almost by accident when creating YouTube tutorials to help his niece in Algebra. His journey slowly unleashed his passion for teaching and revealed the opportunity for him to reach millions of students through the power of the Internet. Compelled by his student's academic struggles and inspired by their successes, he soon became an internationally known ambassador for online learning and eventually education reform. The originating premise of his website was to provide lessons that students can access online and watch at their own pace to augment classroom learning. His lessons, now numbering over four thousand, are short videos covering a specific concept, but now have grown from his niece's algebra to include multiple subject areas ("2014 State of the Valley"). Khan's transformative website began humbly but is now seen as a force for change in education.

While Salman Khan is focused on enhancing early education, Anant Agarwal, founder of edX, has set his sights on higher learning. Speaking at a TED symposium, he describes his desire to "reimagine education" through MOOCs - Massive Open Online Courses available to "students all over the world, in the millions, for free. Anyone who has an Internet connection and the will to learn can access these great courses from excellent universities" (Agarwal, 2013). His company edX is a MOOC provider that assembles and organizes access to available courses, provides online activities to enhance knowledge of the subject matter and, in some cases, provides credentials for students who successfully complete a course. Agarwal sees the opportunity to take education, which has been "calcified for five hundred years," from "oxcarts to airplanes" through the use of online resources (Agarwal, 2103). Perusing the edX website, students can enroll in subjects as diverse as "The Art of Poetry" from Boston University to "Autonomous Navigation for Flying Robots" from Technische Universität München in Germany (edX How it works). Clearly educational opportunity is being drastically changed in its availability and application through these online resources.

There are other foundations similar to Khan Academy and edX, which create, consolidate and organize learning tools. Currently, any student struggling with a concept can Google search for example, "finding the lowest common denominator" and find additional resources on YouTube and beyond. This is the way Bill Gates, while helping his own kids with homework, happened upon Salman Khan's videos and eventually forged a partnership. The availability, accessibility, and universality of online learning resources expands daily.
